# IITA Africa-wide Biological Control Programme — Research Brief

**Subject:** The biological control of the cassava mealybug (*Phenacoccus manihoti*) in
Africa, led by Hans R. Herren at the International Institute of Tropical Agriculture
(IITA), Ibadan, Nigeria, c. 1979–1994.

**Purpose:** Source-checked reference material for biologicalcontrol.org, a folklore /
social archive for programme alumni. This brief separates documented facts from
estimates and contested claims, and flags vivid detail useful for warm prose.

> Note on honesty: Nothing in this brief should be presented as a verbatim quotation from
> a named person unless it is inside quotation marks AND attributed to a cited source. The
> few real quotes below are marked as such. Do not invent quotes or metrics.

---

## 1. Quick orientation — who, what, where, when

- **The pest:** *Phenacoccus manihoti* Matile-Ferrero, the cassava mealybug (CM), a tiny
  sap-sucking scale insect (Homoptera: Pseudococcidae) native to South America. It
  reproduces parthenogenetically (all-female, no mating needed), which is one reason it
  multiplied so explosively in Africa.
- **The crop:** cassava (*Manihot esculenta*), a South American root crop introduced to
  Africa in the 1500s; a staple providing carbohydrates (roots) plus protein and vitamins
  (leaves) for ~200 million Africans at the time.
- **The hero insect:** a parasitoid wasp, *Epidinocarsis lopezi* (De Santis) — later
  reclassified as *Apoanagyrus lopezi*, and today commonly written *Anagyrus lopezi*. About
  the size of a small gnat. A female lays a single egg inside a mealybug; the larva devours
  the host from the inside, "mummifying" it. Host-specific to the cassava mealybug.
- **The institution:** IITA's **Africa-wide Biological Control Programme (ABCP)**, based at
  Ibadan, Nigeria, later with the **Biological Control Centre for Africa (BCCA)** in
  **Cotonou, Benin**.
- **The leader:** **Dr. Hans Rudolf Herren** (b. 30 Nov 1947, Mühleberg, Switzerland),
  Swiss agronomist/entomologist; ETH Zürich PhD in biological control (1977); postdoc at UC
  Berkeley. Arrived at IITA in 1979, aged 31/32.

Sources: World Food Prize 1995 laureate page
(https://www.worldfoodprize.org/en/laureates/19871999_laureates/1995_herren/);
Right Livelihood laureate page
(https://rightlivelihood.org/the-change-makers/find-a-laureate/hans-herren-biovision-foundation/);
Wikipedia, "Hans Rudolf Herren" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Rudolf_Herren);
Neuenschwander & Herren 1988, *Phil. Trans. R. Soc. Lond. B* 318:319–333
(https://doi.org/10.1098/rstb.1988.0012).

## 3. Chronological fact sheet (with citations)

**1500s** — Cassava introduced to Africa from South America (Portuguese trade). Over
centuries it becomes a drought-hardy staple, but arrives *without* its native insect
enemies. [Neuenschwander & Herren 1988; Right Livelihood]

**Early 1970s (reported 1973)** — An unknown mealybug is found devastating cassava in
**Congo (Brazzaville)** and **Zaire (now DR Congo)**. First reports 1973 (Sylvestre;
Hahn & Williams). [Neuenschwander & Herren 1988] Because cassava is propagated from stem
cuttings, the pest almost certainly arrived on plant material carried by people. [swissinfo
interview with Herren, 2011,
https://www.swissinfo.ch/eng/sci-tech/how-a-swiss-scientist-saved-20-million-people/30643902]

**1977** — Matile-Ferrero formally describes the pest as a new species, *Phenacoccus
manihoti*. First biological-control efforts against it begin (per Zeddies et al. 2001).
June 1977: international workshop on the cassava mealybug at M'vuazi, Zaire. [Neuenschwander
& Herren 1988; Zeddies et al. 2001, https://doi.org/10.1016/s0169-5150(00)00064-5]

**1977–1980** — The **Commonwealth Institute of Biological Control (CIBC)** searches the
Caribbean, Venezuela, the Guyanas and NE Brazil for natural enemies. A superficially
similar mealybug is found but turns out to be a *different* species (later named
*P. herreni*), whose parasitoids will not attack *P. manihoti*. A frustrating false start.
[Neuenschwander & Herren 1988]

**1979** — Herren arrives at IITA, Ibadan; the pest reaches Nigeria and Benin. He is
initially the *sole* staff member of the biological control programme. [World Food Prize;
Neuenschwander & Herren 1988]

**1980–1981** — IITA explores the southern USA, Mexico, Central America, northern Colombia
and Venezuela. Herren spends ~18 months traveling Latin American back roads by van,
collecting insects and talking to farmers. [World Food Prize; UPI 1995]

**1981** — **Breakthrough.** *P. manihoti* is finally found at low density in **Paraguay**
by scientists of **CIAT** (Centro Internacional de Agricultura Tropical, Colombia). Among
the parasitoids collected in South America is *Epidinocarsis lopezi*. [Neuenschwander &
Herren 1988; Neuenschwander 2001 review]

**1981 (via London)** — Natural enemies are shipped **via quarantine at CIBC / IIBC,
Silwood Park, England**, reared through a generation, and screened for safety (no
pathogens; no harm to bees or silkworms; host-specificity; and specifically to exclude
hyperparasitoids). Shipments approved by the Inter-African Phytosanitary Council and passed
through Nigerian Plant Quarantine. [Neuenschwander & Herren 1988; Neuenschwander 2001]

**November 1981** — **First release** of *E. lopezi* at IITA/Ibadan (start of the dry
season). About 1,850 wasps released 11/81–1/82 at Ibadan/Ilora. [Neuenschwander & Herren
1988; Neuenschwander 2001, Table 1]

**November 1982** — Second release near **Abeokuta**. The wasp survives the following rainy
season (its hardest test) → **permanent establishment** confirmed. [Neuenschwander & Herren
1988]

**1982–1986** — Further South American exploration in **Paraguay, Bolivia and Mato Grosso
do Sul, Brazil**, funded by German **GTZ** under IITA. Only eight areas with *P. manihoti*
were found, all at very low density — so low that lab-reared mealybugs on trap plants had
to be set out in fields to lure the parasitoids. Two entomologists (incl. B. Löhr and
A. M. Varela) did this work. [Neuenschwander & Herren 1988; Löhr et al. 1990]

**March 1983** — ~10 wasp generations after release, *E. lopezi* recovered from nearly all
fields within 100 km of the release site, and 170 km north into Guinea savannah. [N&H 1988]

**End of 1984** — Three years after first release, *E. lopezi* found in ~70% of all fields
across >200,000 km² of southwestern Nigeria. One of the fastest dispersals ever recorded
for a tiny wasp (microhymenoptera). [N&H 1988]

**1980s (mid)** — Mass rearing scales up; ground and aerial releases roll out across the
cassava belt "on request" from national governments, always with local collaborators.
[Neuenschwander & Herren 1988; Neuenschwander 2001]

**By end of 1986** — Established in **16 countries**, over **~750,000 km²**; releases made
at 50+ sites. [Neuenschwander & Herren 1988]

**1988** — Occurred in **18 countries** over ~1.5 million km². [Neuenschwander & Herren
1989, J. Appl. Entomol. https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1439-0418.1989.tb00475.x]

**1990** — Established in **24 countries**, spread over >12.7 million km² (total area, not
just cassava). [Neuenschwander et al. 1990,
https://biblio.iita.org/documents/S90ArtNeuenschwanderBiologicalInthomDev.pdf]

**1990** — IITA and CIAT awarded the **CGIAR King Baudouin Award**. [ScienceDaily 2010]

**1991** — Landmark review: Herren & Neuenschwander, "Biological control of cassava pests
in Africa," *Annual Review of Entomology* 36:257–283. Reports control achieved in ~95% of
fields by the decade's end. [cited in Zeddies et al. 2001]

**1992** — Herren becomes Director of IITA's Plant Health Management Division. [Right
Livelihood]

**1994** — Last releases made (per Neuenschwander 2001). Herren leaves IITA to become
Director-General of **icipe** (International Centre of Insect Physiology and Ecology),
Nairobi, 1994–2005. [Wikipedia; Right Livelihood]

**1995** — Herren awarded the World Food Prize. [World Food Prize]

**By ~1996/2001** — Programme regarded as complete and among the best-documented biological
control projects ever; established in **26 African countries**. [Neuenschwander 2001,
*Biological Control* 21:214–229]

**2001** — Economic analysis (Zeddies, Schaab, Neuenschwander, Herren), *Agricultural
Economics* 24:209–219: benefit–cost ratio ~200 (world price) to ~370–740 (intra-African
prices). [https://doi.org/10.1016/s0169-5150(00)00064-5]

**2008–2010 (legacy)** — The cassava mealybug invades **Thailand/SE Asia**. IITA (Benin)
and CIAT help; IITA entomologist **Georg Goergen** hand-carries a starter colony of ~500
*A. lopezi* from Benin to Bangkok (2009); Thailand releases the wasp from July 2010. [IITA /
ScienceDaily 2010]

## 4. The pest crisis — why it exploded, and the human stakes

- Cassava reached Africa without its natural enemies. When the mealybug arrived (also
  without *its* natural enemies), it "spread like wildfire." [Herren, swissinfo 2011]
- It reproduces **parthenogenetically** — populations are all-female and explode fast.
  [N&H 1988]
- It attacks the growing tips ("candlestick" / bunchy-top symptom): leaves curl and
  shrivel, shoots become distorted rosettes, plants can be completely defoliated. The UPI
  1995 article calls it a "candlestick disease" before Herren recognized it as insect
  damage. [UPI 1995; N&H 1988]
- **Documented tuber-yield losses up to ~80%** (Nwanze 1982); in the worst areas up to
  four-fifths of the crop ruined. Infested cuttings made poor planting stock, so cassava
  *disappeared* from some regions entirely. [N&H 1988; World Food Prize]
- Peak infestations exceeded **1,000–1,500 mealybugs on a single shoot tip**; a Malawi
  outbreak averaged 554 per tip across 13 fields. [N&H 1988; UCR/Legner]
- Cassava is the drought fallback crop — "the only hope for feeding the continent's people
  in famine situations." Chemical spraying was tried by governments but was expensive,
  impractical across smallholder mixed-cropping, and no lasting fix. [World Food Prize;
  Right Livelihood]

## 5. Finding the wasp in South America

- The logic: cassava is Amazonian, so the pest and its enemies must be South American.
  Search there. [Herren, swissinfo 2011]
- The twist: for years no one had seen this mealybug in South America — because in its
  homeland it is kept so rare by natural enemies. It was eventually found on *cultivated*
  cassava in the border region of **Paraguay, Bolivia and Brazil** (Mato Grosso do Sul).
  [Herren, swissinfo 2011; N&H 1988]
- Populations there were so low (peaks ~16 per tip in dry season, <1 in wet season) that
  researchers had to bait fields with lab-reared mealybugs on potted "trap plants" to catch
  the parasitoids. [N&H 1988]
- The wasp *E. lopezi* had originally been described in 1963 from northern Argentina, from
  an unidentified mealybug. [N&H 1988]
- To boost genetic diversity, wasps were imported to Africa **several times** from both
  Paraguay and Brazil. [Neuenschwander 2001]

> ⚠️ **Contested / commonly-muddled attributions:** Popular accounts (incl. the World Food
> Prize page and Wikipedia) call it a "Paraguayan wasp" and credit Herren personally with
> finding it. The peer-reviewed record is more precise: *P. manihoti* was located in
> Paraguay by **CIAT**; natural enemies (incl. *E. lopezi*) were collected via the **CIBC**
> and later GTZ/IITA exploration teams (e.g., **M. Yaseen**, **B. Löhr**, **A. M. Varela**);
> quarantine screening was done in **London**. Herren *led and built* the programme and
> championed the strategy; he was not literally the lone collector. Present the team story.

## 6. Mass rearing / insectary operations

- *E. lopezi* was (and still is) reared on its only host, the cassava mealybug, feeding on
  **potted cassava plants** — a three-tier living system: plant → mealybug → wasp.
  [N&H 1988]
- Rearing was industrial in scale: **>50 rearing units** with potted plants, plus **large
  mechanized cages with hydroponic cassava cultures** where plant, host and parasitoid were
  reared together in one unit. [Neuenschwander & Herren 1989, J. Appl. Entomol.]
- Biology that made rearing hard (all documented): low fecundity (~40–67 offspring/female,
  max ~10/day), ~10-day adult lifespan, ~2-week development at 27 °C, lower thermal
  threshold 13.3 °C, preference for particular host instars, host-feeding, superparasitism,
  and ~10–16% of parasitoid larvae encapsulated (killed) by the host's immune response.
  [N&H 1988]
- Emphasis was on **quality over quantity**: for "inoculative" releases you don't need
  millions at each site — sometimes fewer than 100 wasps established a new population.
  [N&H 1988; Neuenschwander & Herren 1989]
- Herren grew the ABCP from a one-man operation into a center of **20+ scientists**, and
  trained **850+ experts from 30 countries**. [World Food Prize]

## 7. Release logistics — ground + "wasps from airplanes"

- Releases were done **only on request** by national programmes and **in collaboration with
  local agronomists/entomologists** — a deliberate, respectful modus operandi.
  [Neuenschwander 2001]
- Ground releases: wasps carried in packaging, released at chosen field sites; numbers per
  site ranged from <100 to tens of thousands (see Neuenschwander 2001, Table 1, country by
  country).
- **Aerial release — the famous part.** IITA developed an **Automatic Insect Release System
  (AIRS)**, built with **Firma Dieringer (Austria)** and **Ciba-Pilatus / Zimex Aviation
  (Switzerland)**, installed in a **twin turbo-prop aircraft**, to drop wasps over large,
  hard-to-reach areas. [Cambridge / *Insect Sci. Applic.* 8(4-6):883
  https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/international-journal-of-tropical-insect-science/article/abs/technology-for-automated-aerial-release-of-natural-enemies-of-the-cassava-mealybug-and-cassava-green-mite/8B29F7FC0A3C5DB426BA29F592AE21A9]
- Herren's own vivid description: wasps were "packaged in a way that they could be shot out
  of an airplane travelling at 300 kilometres per hour or more across Africa, from Senegal
  on one side to Mozambique on the other." [Herren, swissinfo 2011 — real quote]
- Packaging/mortality data existed: preliminary AIRS tests showed ~25% died from packaging
  to release and up to 25% during release, with ≥50% surviving. [Cambridge abstract]
- The system also released the predatory mite / other agents for the companion **cassava
  green mite** programme (*Mononychellus tanajoa*). [Cambridge abstract]

> ⚠️ **Contested numbers to handle carefully:**
> - World Food Prize: "By 1986 … almost **2,000 wasps per second**." Striking but a
>   promotional figure — attribute to the World Food Prize, don't state as raw fact.
> - Right Livelihood: "**Some 1.6 million wasps** were released between 1982 and 1993 …
>   in **24 countries**." Use as "according to Right Livelihood."
> - "Within five months … one wasp population had spread over **120 miles**." [World Food
>   Prize] Consistent with the peer-reviewed "100+ km per dry season." [N&H 1988]

## 8. Geographic scale, collaborators, donors

- **Range:** from Senegal/Gambia (west) to Mozambique/Tanzania and the East African
  highlands (east); the cassava belt spans ~15°N to ~20°S. Madagascar was notably *not*
  reached by the pest by the mid-1990s. [N&H 1988; Zeddies et al. 2001]
- **Countries with confirmed establishment/releases** (Neuenschwander 2001, Table 1):
  Benin, Burkina/Niger region, Burundi, Cameroon, Central African Republic, Congo, Côte
  d'Ivoire, Equatorial Guinea, Gabon, Gambia, Ghana, Guinea-Bissau, Guinea-Conakry, Kenya,
  Malawi, Mozambique, Nigeria, Rwanda, Senegal, Sierra Leone, Tanzania (incl. Zanzibar),
  Togo, Uganda, Zaire, Zambia. (Reached **26 countries** total by ~2001.)
- **National collaborators:** the programme's ethic was to work through national programmes;
  many local scientists are co-authors — e.g. **K. M. Lema** (early Nigeria releases),
  **A. H. Bokonon-Ganta** (Benin), **A. R. Cudjoe** and **S. Korang-Amoakoh** (Ghana),
  **A. Kiyindou** (Congo), and government entomologists across the belt.
- **Donors** (named in the papers): Austria, Belgium, Canada (IDRC), Denmark, Germany
  (BMZ/GTZ), Italy, the Netherlands, Norway, Switzerland; plus the **African Development
  Bank**, **European Economic Community**, **International Fund for Agricultural Development
  (IFAD, Rome)**, **UNDP**, **FAO**, and **USAID** (Zaire, Guinea-Bissau). [N&H 1988;
  Neuenschwander 2001 acknowledgments]
- Herren "organized **$20 million over 12 years**" for the programme. [World Food Prize]
  Zeddies et al. put total programme cost (discounted) at ~US$47 million over the full
  1974–2013 analysis window.

## 9. Documented impact / yield / lives — facts vs. estimates

**Well-documented (peer-reviewed):**
- Mealybug damage symptoms in SW Nigeria fell from **88% of plants** (first dry season after
  release) to **23%** the next year; average dropped to ~11 mealybugs per tip. [N&H 1988]
- Control achieved in **~95% of fields** by the end of the 1980s. [Herren & Neuenschwander
  1991, via Zeddies et al. 2001]
- Exclusion experiments proved the wasp — not weather or native predators — was the key
  factor. [N&H 1988; Neuenschwander 2001]
- No documented resurgence; equilibrium has held for **30+ years**. [Neuenschwander 2001;
  Herren, swissinfo 2011]
- **Benefit–cost ratio ~200** (cassava at world price) up to **~370–740** (intra-African
  prices); internal rate of return ~200–300%. [Zeddies et al. 2001] Annual benefit
  estimated ~US$235 million; cumulative benefits US$9.4–20+ billion over 40 years depending
  on scenario. [Zeddies et al. 2001]

**Estimate / promotional (attribute, don't assert):**
- "**Saved upward of 20 million lives**" — this is the **World Food Prize / biography**
  framing (Herren's German biography is literally titled *How Hans Rudolf Herren saved 20
  million people*). It is an **estimate of famine averted**, widely repeated (Wikipedia,
  Right Livelihood, swissinfo), NOT a body count. Always phrase as "credited with" /
  "estimated to have helped avert a famine that could have cost ~20 million lives."
- Cassava fed **~200 million people**; provided up to **50% of daily calories** for
  subsistence farmers. [World Food Prize; UPI 1995]

> ⚠️ The "20 million lives" figure is the single most-repeated and most-contestable claim.
> It is an estimate of a catastrophe *prevented*, not a measured statistic. Treat with care
> and attribution. It is not fabricated — it originates with the World Food Prize
> Foundation — but it is not a hard number.

## 11. Vivid concrete details (for warm, readable prose)

- The wasp is roughly gnat-sized and lays **one egg inside one mealybug**; the victim
  becomes a "mummy." A dramatic David-and-Goliath image at insect scale.
- The mealybug is a she-only army: **no males needed** to reproduce. (Good for a wry line.)
- The disease that wasn't a disease: farmers and officials described a "**candlestick**"
  affliction — leaves gone, only the bare stalk standing like a candle — before Herren
  realized it was a bug, not a pathogen. [UPI 1995]
- **18 months** of Herren "jouncing in vans along back roads in Latin America, talking to
  farmers, collecting insects and placating soldiers." His own words: "Sometimes it was a
  bit dicey, and sometimes it was a great deal of fun." [UPI 1995 — real quote]
- The needle-in-a-haystack irony: the pest was **invisible in its homeland** precisely
  because nature already controlled it — they had to bait fields with lab mealybugs to lure
  the wasps out. [N&H 1988; swissinfo]
- **Quarantine in London** (Silwood Park): every wasp checked so it wouldn't harm bees or
  silkworms, and to make sure no "hyperparasitoids" (parasites of the parasite) sneaked in.
- The **flying insectary**: a twin turbo-prop plane with a custom Austrian/Swiss-built
  "Automatic Insect Release System," dispensing wasps at ~300 km/h. [Cambridge; swissinfo]
- Scale poetry: the World Food Prize's "**almost 2,000 wasps per second**" line.
- Speed: a wasp population spreading **100+ km in a single dry season** — one of the fastest
  wasp dispersals ever recorded. [N&H 1988]
- Cost poetry (Zeddies et al.): spread over the whole cassava belt and 40 years, the
  programme cost about **13 US cents per hectare per year** — for a benefit-cost ratio of
  ~200-plus.
- The **"potted-plant" food chain**: to raise wasps you first raise mealybugs, and to raise
  mealybugs you first raise cassava — a living Russian doll in the insectary.
- Human touch of the legacy: in 2009, IITA's Georg Goergen literally **carried ~500 wasps by
  hand from Benin to Bangkok** to start Thailand's rescue. [ScienceDaily 2010]
- Herren was **31 and essentially the whole programme** when he started; a decade later it
  was a continent-spanning operation of 20+ scientists.
